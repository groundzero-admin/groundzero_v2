The Five Signal Sources — Ground Zero's Data Architecture

Two confirmed facts — live facilitation with full transcripts, and an AI companion with full chat logs — fundamentally upgrade Ground Zero's signal architecture. What was previously a measurement problem for two of the four pillars is now solvable from day one.

📋
Session Transcript
Full verbatim log of every live facilitated session. Every student utterance, facilitator probe, peer exchange.
🤖
SPARK Chat
Student↔AI companion conversations. Free-range, student-initiated exploration. Richest curiosity signal available.
✅
MCQ + Short Answer
Structured in-session responses. Correct/incorrect + response time. Direct BKT input for Math/Logic and AI/Systems.
👤
Facilitator Note
Minimal post-session annotation. Now scoped to only what transcripts cannot capture — engagement quality and edge-case flags.
🛠️
Project Artifact
Product cards, pitch decks, vision boards, system maps. Longitudinal builder mindset signal. Evaluated at Demo Day.
The critical shift from v1: In the original signal design, Communication and Creativity/Curiosity were rated as "hardest to measure" because they required either NLP on sparse short-answer text or heavy facilitator annotation. Full session transcripts solve the first problem — every spoken argument, every peer interaction, every instance of a student explaining their reasoning is now in a log. SPARK chat transcripts solve the second — curiosity measured in a structured activity is artificial; curiosity measured in a free-range AI conversation is real. Both pillars are now as measurable as Math/Logic and AI/Systems.

Part A — The Signal Taxonomy

Signals are now organised by source rather than by explicit/implicit — because the distinction between what you can and can't measure has fundamentally changed with transcripts. Each source contributes a different type of evidence, and the combination is what powers the learning graph.

Source 1: Session Transcripts SESSION TRANSCRIPT

Source 2: SPARK Companion Chat Transcripts SPARK CHAT

SPARK chat is qualitatively different from every other signal source. It's the only place where student behaviour is entirely self-directed — there's no facilitator guiding the conversation, no curriculum structure, no right answer being sought. What a student chooses to do with SPARK, and how, is as close as you can get to observing genuine intrinsic motivation.

Why SPARK transcripts solve the Creativity/Curiosity measurement problem: In structured activities, curiosity is confounded by the task — a student asking a question might be trying to get the right answer rather than genuinely wondering. In SPARK chat, there is no right answer. A student who spends 15 minutes exploring "what would happen if AI could feel emotions" is demonstrating something that no quiz can measure. This is your most distinctive signal.

Source 4: Facilitator Notes FACILITATOR NOTE

With transcripts and SPARK covering most of what facilitators previously had to report, the annotation burden shrinks dramatically. The facilitator form now covers only the three things that live observation captures and transcripts cannot: genuine engagement quality, spontaneous notable moments, and intervention flags.

Design principle that doesn't change: Every field in the facilitator form must be a tap, not a type. The one free-text field is optional and reserved for genuinely notable moments that no algorithm will catch — the kind of thing a great teacher would remember and tell a parent.
#What to captureFormatWhy transcripts alone can't cover this
1 Genuine engagement vs. going through the motions — per student One tap per student: ✓ Engaged / ~ Partial / ✗ Motions A student can speak frequently in a transcript and still be disengaged. Body language, eye contact, and energy are invisible to NLP.
2 Notable moment flag — "did I witness something today worth remembering?" Optional free-text, one per session. Name + what happened. The highest-quality signal in your system. These annotated moments become your gold-standard training examples for every model you build.
3 Intervention flag — is this student struggling in a way that warrants a parent or support conversation? Binary flag per student. Triggers a review queue. NLP can detect consistent low performance, but cannot detect when a student seems fine on paper but something is clearly off. This is the human override signal.

Part B — Signals Mapped to the Four Skill Pillars

The key change from v1: every pillar now has at least two strong primary sources. No pillar is dependent on a single signal type. Each pillar box below shows which sources are primary vs. supporting, and which specific signals from each source are most diagnostic.

🔴 Pillar 1: Communication + Argumentation

v2 status: Fully measurable from transcripts + short answers. Facilitator annotation is now supplementary, not required. The transcript provides a continuous, comparable record of how each student's spoken communication evolves across sessions — something no previous edtech tool has had at this granularity.

Primary sources

  • TRANSCRIPT Response elaboration depth after facilitator probes
  • TRANSCRIPT Position change with vs. without justification
  • TRANSCRIPT Audience adaptation quality (semantic divergence across audience versions)
  • TRANSCRIPT Peer-building vs. peer-echoing behaviour

  • ARTIFACT Pitch deck argument structure at Demo Day

🔵 Pillar 2: Creativity + Curiosity

v2 status: SPARK transcripts solve this pillar. This was the hardest to measure in v1. It is now the pillar with the most distinctive and authentic signal — because SPARK captures curiosity in its natural state, not triggered by a task. The session transcript adds the group-context creativity signals (originality vs peers, novel idea contribution).

Primary sources

  • SPARK Multi-turn depth on single topic — depth of curiosity
  • SPARK Hypothetical / counterfactual framing ("what if")
  • SPARK Cross-session topic recurrence — sustained curiosity
  • SPARK Emotional engagement markers (surprise, excitement, wonder)

Remaining open questions

QuestionStatusWhy it matters
Are short answer responses question-anchored or fully free-form? OPEN Question-anchored responses are significantly easier to score automatically — you normalise against a known good answer for that specific question. Free-form needs a more general reasoning scorer, which requires ~3× more training data.
What is the typical student return frequency? (Weekly? 2×/week?) OPEN Forgetting curve decay parameters depend on inter-session gap. A student returning twice a week needs a very different review scheduling model than one returning every two weeks.
Is SPARK chat free-range or structured? (Can students ask it anything, or is it bounded to curriculum topics?) OPEN Free-range SPARK is a far richer curiosity signal. If SPARK is bounded to curriculum, the out-of-curriculum interest mapping analysis doesn't apply — though in-curriculum depth and push-back detection still do.
Are transcripts speaker-diarised? (Does the system already label which words belong to which student vs facilitator?) OPEN Most transcript NLP analyses require knowing which words are the student's. If diarisation isn't built in, it needs to be added before the transcript analysis pipeline is useful.
The updated core bet: Ground Zero now has five signal sources — session transcripts, SPARK chat, MCQ logs, facilitator notes, and project artifacts — that together cover all four skill pillars without a single measurement gap. No competitor has full session transcripts + AI companion chat logs for this age group, annotated against a capability-based rubric. The moat is not the learning graph algorithm. The moat is this dataset, built one cohort at a time, starting now.

Part E — Onboarding Diagnostic: Seeding the Learning Graph

Before a student attends their first session, the learning graph has no data. This section defines a 20-question onboarding conversation — delivered over voice or text via SPARK — that places each student at an initial competency level across all four pillars. It becomes the seed from which every subsequent BKT update grows.

The core design principle: These questions do not test whether a student knows their curriculum. They use curriculum knowledge the student already has as a vehicle to reveal how they think. A grade 8 student explaining fraction addition to a younger child is not being assessed on fractions — they're revealing their Communication depth, their conceptual vs. procedural math understanding, and their ability to decompose a system. The question uses known content to surface unknown thinking.
~20 min

Delivered conversationally over voice or text via SPARK before Session 1. Feels like a chat, not a test.

4 seeds

Outputs an initial P(L) estimate for each pillar — the starting competency web node for the learning graph.

Grade-adaptive

Curriculum anchors shift per grade band so questions feel natural and appropriately challenging — never too easy, never intimidating.

What the onboarding produces for each student

🔴 Communication: Stage 1–5 placement

Based on how they explain, whether they give reasons, how they respond to challenge, and whether they adapt their language to the prompt's framing.

🔵 Creativity: Stage 1–5 placement

Based on idea fluency, whether they generate unexpected answers, how they engage with open-ended prompts, and whether they show genuine curiosity vs. task completion.

🟢 AI/Systems: Stage 1–5 placement

Based on mental models of how systems work, cause-effect thinking, product instinct, and any prior AI interaction behaviour.

🟣 Math/Logic: Stage 1–5 placement

Based on conceptual depth behind procedural knowledge, pattern recognition, probabilistic reasoning, and whether they distinguish correlation from causation.

Questions are distributed across pillars but no question tests only one. The table shows each question's primary pillar (what it's primarily designed to reveal) and secondary pillars (what strong responses also surface). The scoring is not pass/fail — every response places the student somewhere on a 1–5 scale for each relevant pillar.

Grade 4–5  ·  Ages 9–11

Curriculum anchors: multiplication, basic fractions, simple geometry, life sciences, basic probability, sequencing, cause & effect
# Question Curriculum anchor Pillars Strong response signals Watch-out signals
1 "If you had to explain what multiplication really means — not how to calculate it, but what it is — to a 5-year-old, what would you say?" Multiplication as repeated addition / grouping (Gr 3–4) MathComm Uses analogy ("5 groups of 3 apples"). Distinguishes meaning from procedure. Chooses age-appropriate language. Recites times tables. Says "it's like adding but faster" without explaining the grouping concept.
2 "Your friend says 3/8 is bigger than 1/2 because 3 is bigger than 1. How would you convince them they're wrong?" Fraction comparison (Gr 3–4) MathComm Uses "of the whole" language, draws picture, converts to common denominator, or uses pizza/pie analogy. Addresses why the friend is wrong, not just that they are. "You're just wrong" or restates the correct answer without an explanation the friend could follow.
3 "If you flip a coin 10 times and get tails every single time, what do you think is more likely on flip 11 — heads or tails? Why?" Basic probability, independence (Gr 4) MathComm "Still 50/50 — the coin doesn't remember what happened." Articulates independence intuitively even without the term. "Heads, because it's overdue" (gambler's fallacy) — note as a specific misconception, not a general weakness.
4 "Give me 5 completely different ways to use a cardboard box that have nothing to do with storing things." None — pure curiosity probe Curiosity Specific and personal question — one they've clearly thought about before. Even better: shows they've already tried to find an answer. "Nothing" or very generic/borrowed question ("what's the meaning of life") — suggests curiosity hasn't been cultivated or they're not comfortable revealing it yet.

("It lives in very cold water, so it has thick fur and eats slow-moving fish.") Adaptation logic is present even if not labelled as such. Random assembly of features with no internal logic ("it has wings and eats rocks and glows") — creativity without systems thinking.

Instruction-giving, sequencing AI/SysComm Instructions are step-by-step and specific. Vague high-level description ("just do it nicely") with no awareness that robots need precise instructions.

Metacognition — any curriculum topic CuriosityComm Genuine example with a specific before/after. Can articulate what changed their thinking (new information, experience, argument from someone). Shows self-awareness of learning. "I can't think of anything" or a very vague answer — suggests low metacognitive awareness at this stage.

10 "How would you explain the difference between a 'hypothesis' and a 'guess' to someone who thinks they're the same thing?" Scientific method (Gr 6) CommLogic "A hypothesis is based on what you already know — it's a specific, testable prediction, not random." 11 "If a = 3 and b = 5, write three different equations that are true. Now deliberately write one that is false — and explain why it's false." Deliberately constructs a false equation and explains the violation — shows understanding of equality as a condition, not just a calculation. Articulates why biased inputs create unfair outputs — even if they're "predictive." "Just use their grades" — correct but incomplete. Misses the fairness dimension entirely.
14 "Pick any historical figure you've studied. What's the one thing they'd be most confused by if they saw the world today — and what would you tell them?" History curriculum (any) CommCreativity Specific, insightful choice that reveals genuine understanding of both the historical figure's worldview and the present. The explanation to the figure is calibrated to their context, not our vocabulary. "They'd be amazed by phones / the internet" — surface-level observation requiring no understanding of the historical figure's specific perspective.
15 "Why is the area of a circle πr²? Don't just state the formula — explain why it has to be that shape of formula." Systems design, fairness SystemsLogic Considers multiple inputs, consistency across students, an appeals process. None — product thinking + self-awareness BuilderCreativity Specific and personal "know" items (not generic: "my schedule," "when I'm tired," "my actual interests not just my age"). Statistics: central tendency (Gr 7–8) MathComm Explains use cases: mean for symmetric data, median when there are outliers (income distribution), mode for categorical data. Gives concrete example of when mean is misleading. Correctly defines all three but can't explain when each is appropriate — shows procedural knowledge without applied judgment.

Crucially: separates factual from normative disagreement — "they agree on what's true but disagree on what matters most." Biology: photosynthesis (Gr 7–8) SystemsLogic "The plant needs glucose for energy and growth — oxygen is a byproduct, not the goal." Finds 2 but can't explain why no other even primes exist — treats it as a memorised exception rather than a logical consequence.

Directed numbers, algebra (Gr 7–8) MathComm Pattern argument: 3×–2=–6, 2×–2=–4, 1×–2=–2, 0×–2=0, so –1×–2 must follow the pattern = +2. Shows epistemological sophistication — "the test is measuring something, but is that something empathy?" "That's amazing" (accepts uncritically) or "AI can't have feelings" (correct conclusion, no argument) — both fail to engage with the measurement problem.

Scientific method: controlled experiments, medicine (Gr 8–9) CommLogic Explains the psychological effect on symptoms (belief changes biology), explains why you need a control group to isolate the drug's effect, and shows that without a placebo you can't know whether the drug worked or whether believing it would work did. 20 "What's a belief you held confidently a year or two ago that you've since completely changed? What changed it — and how do you now think about trusting your own beliefs?" Metacognition, epistemology CuriosityComm Genuine, personal example. Can articulate the mechanism of change (new evidence, different perspective, lived experience). "I used to like X food and now I don't" — trivial, avoids genuine epistemic reflection. Or "I haven't really changed my mind on anything important" — either low self-awareness or reluctance to be vulnerable.
On scoring: No single question produces a definitive pillar placement. Each question produces a 1–3 evidence signal per relevant pillar. The BKT P(L) seed for each pillar is the weighted average across the 5–7 questions that tested it, informed by the signal quality from the response. Questions 17 and 20 (in each band) are the most diagnostic for Creativity/Curiosity and should be weighted more heavily for that pillar. Response time adds a cognitive load signal to all MCQ-style questions.
What this unlocks for session design: A student who enters at Creativity Stage 4 but Communication Stage 2 should receive a very different first session experience from one at the reverse. The onboarding diagnostic turns the learning graph from a system that needs data to start working into a system that starts working on day one — which is the cold-start problem solved.
